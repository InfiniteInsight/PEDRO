Quick context

What is executive function

It's the part of your brain that handles getting things done. Starting tasks. Staying organized. Managing your time. Not losing your keys. Regulating your emotions so one bad thing doesn't ruin the whole day.

When it's working, you don't notice it. When it's not, everything falls apart. And it gets worse when you're tired, stressed, burned out, or sad.

Let's talk

Have you ever known exactly what you needed to do, but you just... couldn't?

That gap between knowing and doing. That's executive function. What does that feel like for you?

Being honest

The stuff nobody says

  • Most of these apps don't have evidence they actually work. A review of 109 of them found zero with real proof. ScienceDirect/PubMed, 2020
  • Streaks and badges can create guilt instead of motivation. You miss a day and you feel worse, not better.
  • Every new app is another thing you have to manage. If using the tool requires the thing you're struggling with, it's not helping.
  • But: tools that show up at the right time, in the right way, for people who actually need them? Those do show real results. Shepard et al., 2023; Frontiers in Education, 2025
People in this space

Worth looking up

  • Dr. K / Healthy Gamer GG – Harvard psychiatrist, Twitch streamer, was addicted to games himself. Runs EF coaching groups. Accessible and real. healthygamer.gg
  • Brené Brown – Shame and perfectionism make executive function worse. Her burnout episode with the Nagoski sisters is worth a listen.
  • Emily & Amelia Nagoski – Wrote Burnout. Removing the stressor isn't enough, your body still needs to complete the stress cycle.
  • Dr. Gloria Mark (UC Irvine) – Studies attention. 23+ min to refocus after an interruption. Wrote Attention Span.
Our constraint

If using the app requires executive function, we failed.

So how do we make something that's simple enough to use on your worst day, but smart enough to actually help?

Slide 3

The Winning Pitch

~5 min
This is the actual text from the winning submission. Go through each piece. The pitch is solid because it answers the basic questions: what, who, how, what's different, how it reaches you.
"Take the pressure off our partners" is the emotional core. It's about the person who has to remind you, check on you, ask if you did the thing. That relationship tax.
"Adjusts if today you're sad" is the differentiator. Most planners treat every day the same. A bad sleep Tuesday isn't the same as a good energy Tuesday.
"Can text you" is interesting because it means the app comes to you. You don't have to remember to open it. That's a design choice worth unpacking.

Slide 4

Discussion: Partners

~8 min
This is the first big discussion. Let it breathe. The pitch specifically said "partners" and that resonated enough to win the vote. Why?
It could mean romantic partners, spouses, roommates, parents, coworkers. Anyone who ends up picking up slack or being the reminder system for someone else.
Let people share if they want. This might get personal and that's okay. Some people are the ones who struggle with this, some are the partners. Both perspectives are useful for designing the app.
Listen

Your job here is to facilitate, not lecture. Ask follow-ups. "What does that look like day to day?" "How does that feel on both sides?" Take notes on what people say; it's design research.

Slide 5

What is EF

~2 min
Keep this casual. You're not giving a medical definition. You're just making sure everyone's on the same page about what we're talking about.
The key thing: executive function gets worse when you're tired, stressed, sad, or burned out. It's not a fixed trait. It fluctuates. That's why the winning pitch said "adjusts if today you're sad."
If anyone wants to go deeper: Dr. K on Healthy Gamer GG has great accessible explainers. Dr. Russell Barkley's free fact sheets at russellbarkley.org are the clinical standard.

Slide 6

Discussion: The Gap

~6 min
Second big discussion. "Have you ever known exactly what you needed to do but couldn't make yourself do it?" Basically everyone has experienced this. It's not just a neurodivergent thing.
The point isn't to diagnose anyone or make this clinical. It's to make the problem concrete so we can design for it. What does that stuck feeling actually feel like? What breaks the logjam? What makes it worse?
Whatever people share here is design input. If someone says "I just need someone to tell me the first step," that's a feature. If someone says "I need someone to not judge me," that's a tone decision for the AI.
Listen + Note

Write down what people say. This feeds directly into the polls later.

Slide 8

Discussion: What Works/Doesn't

~8 min
Third big discussion, and probably the most useful one. Let people share their personal app/system graveyard. Everyone has one. The to-do app they used for 3 days. The planner they bought and never opened. The habit tracker that made them feel bad.
Listen for patterns: What made them try it? What made them stop? Was it too complicated? Too many notifications? Not enough? Made them feel guilty? Forgot it existed?
Also listen for what DID stick. If someone says "I've used the same app for 2 years," ask them why. That's gold for our design.
Design Research

Slide 9

Being Honest

~3 min
Don't dwell here. Just be straight about it: most of these apps don't have evidence they work. We should know that going in.
The 0/109 stat is from a systematic review in ScienceDirect/PubMed (2020). None of 109 ADHD mobile apps provided scientific evidence for effectiveness.
But: tools designed for people who actually need them, that show up at the right moment, do show real results. A 2023 review (Shepard et al.) found medium-to-large effects for clinical populations. An 8-week study in Frontiers in Education (2025) found gamified tools improved real outcomes and the improvements stuck.
The framing: we're not going to pretend our app cures anything. We're building a tool. A tool that might help someone get through a hard day. That's enough.
ScienceDirect/PubMed 2020; Shepard et al. 2023; Frontiers in Education 2025; FTC v. Lumosity 2016; Stanford/Max Planck consensus 2014

Slide 10

People Worth Knowing

~3 min
Reference slide. Don't lecture on each person. Just give enough for people to look them up later if they're interested.
Dr. K: The one to highlight most. His content is accessible, he's been through it himself, and he literally runs EF coaching groups. If anyone wants to understand this space better, start with his YouTube.
Brené Brown: Her point about shame is relevant. When you feel shame about not getting things done, it makes it harder to get things done. Vicious cycle. Her podcast episode with the Nagoski sisters on burnout is directly relevant to what we're building.
Nagoski sisters: Key idea from Burnout: the stress response is a cycle. Removing the stressor doesn't complete the cycle. Your body still needs to process it through movement, breathing, crying, creative expression, etc. An app that only manages tasks but ignores how you feel is missing this.
Gloria Mark: 23 minutes to refocus after an interruption. Average screen attention span down to 47 seconds. Wrote Attention Span (2023). Her data is useful context for why notification-heavy apps backfire.

The Constraint

~5 min
This is the design principle for the whole week. Read it, let it land, then open it up.
"If using the app requires executive function, we failed." That means: if someone has to navigate menus, remember to open it, figure out a complex interface, configure settings... we've already lost the people we're building for.
The question on screen is real: how do we make something simple enough for your worst day, but smart enough to actually be useful?
